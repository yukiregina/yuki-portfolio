Problem

The journey that nobody owned

Product Catalogue helps resellers find and promote products, enabling them to showcase independently. But there was a critical gap: the Selling Story journey. Without it, resellers couldn't complete the Catalogue Registration Kit, blocking the entire OKR.

There was no PM at the start. The flow had never been designed, and nobody had connected the dots between what the business needed and what the team could build.

Solution

Design the missing piece, unlock the whole

I identified the gap and stepped into the space left by the missing PM. Working directly with the GPM and key stakeholders, I mapped the requirements and designed the Selling Story journey from scratch alongside the engineering team: 2 frontend developers, 1 backend developer, and a tech lead. A new PM joined later in the process and we shipped it together.

My Approach

Define the flow first, then build.

With no PM in place, I started by working directly with the GPM and stakeholders to understand business requirements and constraints before any screen design. That alignment session became the foundation for the entire flow. Once the logic was agreed, I brought it to the engineering team to stress-test technical feasibility and lock the structure before moving to high-fidelity.

Early alignment: sketching the flow structure with the team before touching Figma

Design Decisions Under Constraint

Reuse first. Build new only when necessary.

The Q4 deadline was fixed. To make it achievable, I made a deliberate decision early: wherever possible, use components the engineering team already knew. The Product Catalogue already had tables and filters that worked well. Rather than designing new patterns, I mapped the Selling Story flow onto those existing components and iterated closely with engineers throughout. The team didn't need to learn anything new: they just needed to apply what was already there to a new context. That decision reduced handoff friction and kept the build on schedule.

Selling Story journey: prototype walkthrough through the full flow

Usability Testing

Tested, learned, changed, then shipped.

I tested the Selling Story journey with 10 participants using a mixed-method approach: remote unmoderated sessions via Maze and 4 moderated in-person interviews. The overall CES (Customer Effort Score) came back at 6.8 out of 7, which meant the flow was working. But the in-person sessions gave me something the Maze data couldn't: I could watch where attention went and hear what people said out loud.

One thing came up consistently: users paused on the list page looking for context. They wanted to know who had made a change and when. In a B2B flow where resellers and promoters are working across the same catalogue, that information isn't a nice-to-have: it's how people orient themselves before acting. I added the actor and timestamp to the list view before handoff. A small change, but one that came directly from watching real people use the product, not from assumption.

Flow Architecture

A single source of truth for the whole team

I structured the flow architecture deliberately so frontend and backend could develop in parallel — critical for hitting the Q4 deadline. By working closely with the dev team throughout implementation, we eliminated the need for separate design reviews at completion. The flow file became the shared reference for the entire sprint: two frontend developers, one backend developer, the tech lead, and eventually the new PM who joined mid-project.

Go-to-Market

Shipping wasn't enough. Adoption had to be earned.

Recognising that a feature only delivers impact if people use it, I developed targeted campaigns for internal teams and external distributors to drive awareness and engagement with the new registration capabilities. Adoption was part of the brief I set for myself, not just delivery.

What I saw coming

The next opportunity, scoped before I shipped the first.

While building the Selling Story, I ran parallel research: interviews and system mapping to understand where the next real opportunity lived. That work surfaced a gap the brief hadn't addressed. To make the OKR truly completable at scale, three areas needed to connect: the Product Catalogue Registration Kit, Personalised Pricing, and a structured Launch Module. Key Learnings

What this project taught me

  • A high usability score doesn't mean you're done learning. The 6.8/7 CES told me the flow worked. The in-person interviews told me what was still missing. Both were necessary.
  • In B2B contexts, context is everything. Users don't just need to complete a task: they need to understand the state of a shared system before they act. The actor and timestamp addition came from watching that need in real time, not from a requirements doc.
  • Stepping into a PM gap is only valuable if you can also step back. Defining the flow early, then handing it off cleanly when the new PM arrived, was as important as building it in the first place.
  • Mapping the full system before touching anything is its own deliverable. I identified the Launch Management Module as a missing piece and handed it over as a structured proposal before I left the team. Whether it was built isn't something I control. Seeing it and naming it clearly is.
